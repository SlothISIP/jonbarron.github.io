Overcoming Data Scarcity in Non-Contact Respiratory Monitoring:
A DTW-Enhanced CNN-LSTM Approach

Abstract


We propose a CNN-LSTM hybrid architecture with DTW-based SMOTE data augmentation for UWB radar respiratory monitoring, achieving 94.3% accuracy with minimal training data. This approach addresses the challenge of limited labeled datasets in medical monitoring applications. Our system demonstrates robust performance in distinguishing normal breathing from apnea events using non-contact ultra-wideband (UWB) radar technology. The integration of Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) with Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE) enables effective data augmentation for time-series respiratory signals, while the CNN-LSTM architecture efficiently captures both spatial and temporal patterns in the radar data. Experimental results show that our method significantly outperforms traditional machine learning approaches and standard deep learning models when training data is scarce, making it particularly suitable for real-world medical monitoring scenarios where collecting large labeled datasets is challenging and expensive.

CNN-LSTM Architecture


Our proposed hybrid architecture combines Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) for spatial feature extraction from UWB radar signals with Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks for temporal sequence modeling. The CNN layers process the radar spectrograms to identify local patterns indicative of respiratory events, while the LSTM layers capture the temporal dependencies and dynamics of breathing patterns over time. This combination enables the model to learn both instantaneous signal characteristics and long-term breathing behavior patterns, leading to improved classification accuracy even with limited training samples.

DTW-Based Data Augmentation


A key innovation in our approach is the use of Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) distance metrics within the SMOTE algorithm for generating synthetic training samples. Traditional SMOTE uses Euclidean distance, which is not well-suited for time-series data with temporal variations. By incorporating DTW, our augmentation method better accounts for the elastic nature of respiratory signals, where the same breathing pattern may occur at different speeds or with slight temporal shifts. This DTW-enhanced SMOTE generates more realistic synthetic samples that preserve the temporal characteristics of respiratory signals, leading to improved model generalization despite limited training data. Classification Performance

Our experimental evaluation demonstrates that the proposed DTW-enhanced CNN-LSTM approach achieves 94.3% classification accuracy in distinguishing normal breathing from apnea events using UWB radar data. This represents a significant improvement over baseline methods:

  • Non-augmented CNN-LSTM: 82.3% accuracy
  • Traditional machine learning (SVM, Random Forest): 82-85% accuracy
  • LSTM-only architecture: 88.9% accuracy
  • CNN-only architecture: 86.2% accuracy

The confusion matrix above shows the detailed classification performance across different respiratory states. Our model achieves high precision and recall for both normal breathing and apnea detection, with minimal false positives and false negatives. This balanced performance is crucial for clinical applications where both types of errors have significant implications for patient care.

ROC Analysis

The Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curves demonstrate the superior discriminative ability of our DTW-enhanced CNN-LSTM model across different operating points. The Area Under the Curve (AUC) of 0.969 indicates excellent classification performance. The curves show that our method maintains high true positive rates while keeping false positive rates low across various threshold settings, providing flexibility for deployment in different clinical scenarios with varying sensitivity requirements.

Ablation Study

The ablation study systematically evaluates the contribution of each component in our proposed framework. Results show that both the CNN-LSTM architecture and DTW-based SMOTE augmentation are essential for achieving optimal performance. Removing the DTW component from SMOTE reduces accuracy by approximately 5.2% (from 94.3% to 89.1%), while using only CNN or LSTM alone results in 8.1% and 5.4% accuracy drops respectively. This analysis confirms that the synergy between our architectural design and data augmentation strategy is key to overcoming data scarcity challenges in respiratory monitoring applications.
